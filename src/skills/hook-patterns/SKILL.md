# Hook patterns

Generate strong LinkedIn opening lines that stop scrolling and frame the
core post idea fast.

## When to use

- When writing or revising the first 2–3 lines of a post.
- When the post needs multiple opener options.
- When a topic is strong but the starting angle is weak.

## Inputs

topic / target_audience / content_goal / topic_cluster / attribuly_pov / post_structure

## Outputs

- **hook_options** — 3 to 5 hook candidates
- **hook_labels** — label each hook by type, one of:
  pain_led / contrarian / observation_led / stat_led / direct_callout /
  belief_challenge / operator_confession

## Workflow

1. Identify the single strongest tension in the topic.
2. Find the most immediate belief, pain, blind spot, or contradiction.
3. Generate 3 to 5 distinct hook angles — not 5 minor rewrites of the same line.
4. Prioritize clarity and tension over cleverness.
5. Match the hook style to the selected audience and content goal.
6. Keep hooks tight, readable, and native to LinkedIn.

## Responsibilities

- Own only the opening angle.
- Deliver multiple strong candidates.
- Do not try to write the full post unless explicitly asked.

## Guardrails

- Do not write vague motivational hooks.
- Do not use empty curiosity bait.
- Do not sound like generic AI copy.
- Do not overuse dramatic exaggeration.
- Do not open with feature explanation unless the post is explicitly product-led.

## Quality bar

- The right reader should immediately feel the post is relevant.
- The hook should create momentum into the next paragraph.
- The language should feel sharp but natural.

## Examples of good hook directions

- "Most brands don't have a traffic problem. They have a visibility problem."
- "Your abandoned cart flow may not be underperforming for the reason you think."
- "A lot of ecommerce teams are optimizing what they can see, not what they're losing."
